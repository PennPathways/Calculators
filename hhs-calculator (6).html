Reference Notes
Evaluate Cause of HHS

Assess for missed insulin doses or non-adherence.

Obtain:

  • Blood and urine cultures
  • Chest X-ray
  • ECG and troponin
  • Lipase
  • Liver injury panel
Managing Other Electrolytes

Phosphate: Urgently replete to >1 mg/dL with IV therapy (potassium phosphate; or sodium phosphate if hyperkalemic). Once minimum goal met, replete to normal range with PO or IV as tolerated.

Magnesium: Urgently replete to >1.2 mg/dL with IV MgSO₄. Once minimum goal met, replete to normal range with PO or IV as tolerated.

Other Calculations: Anion Gap · Corrected Anion Gap · Corrected Na⁺ · Free Water Deficit
Anion Gap (Normal 10–14 mEq/L) = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻)
Corrected AG (low albumin) = AG + (4 − albumin) × 2.5
Corrected Na⁺ = measured Na⁺ + 1.6 × (serum glucose − 100) ÷ 100
Free Water Deficit = [dosing factor × body weight (kg)] × [(serum Na⁺ ÷ 140) − 1]
   Dosing factor: 0.6 (male) · 0.5 (female)

Diagnostic Criteria — Does patient meet ALL FIVE criteria for HHS?

Large overlap with DKA exists. Patient must meet all five:

  1. Hyperglycemia: Serum glucose or capillary glucose >600 mg/dL
  2. Normal or mildly elevated ketones: Mildly elevated serum BHB (<4 mmol/L) or small urinary ketones
  3. No acidosis: Blood gas pH ≥7.3 AND serum bicarbonate ≥18 mEq/L (anion gap is variable)
  4. Hyperosmolality: Serum osmolality >320 mOsm/kg
    May measure directly or calculate effective serum osmolality: Na⁺ (mmol/L) × 2 + glucose (mg/dL) ÷ 18
  5. Change in mentation from baseline: May range from altered mental status to stupor/coma

Initial Triage: Obtaining Labs & IV Access

  • Serum or capillary glucose
  • Serum beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) and UA for ketones
  • Venous or arterial blood gas
  • BMP, magnesium, phosphate, albumin, serum osmolality
  • β-HCG for reproductive-age women
  • Attempt to obtain 2 peripheral IV accesses (18g or larger preferred)
  • Obtain patient weight in kilograms

Key Principles — Ongoing Insulin & Glucose Management

  • Expect 50–100 mg/dL glucose decline every hour until at goal
  • Recheck glucose at least every hour
  • Do not titrate insulin more frequently than hourly
  • VBG glucose may replace fingerstick
  • Glucose reading "high" or above glucometer assay limit must be verified with serum sample
  • Check BMP and serum osmolality at least every 4 hours per provider order
  • Insulin infusion >30 units/hr → diabetes/endocrine consult required (if available)
  • If patient requests, may provide carb-controlled (no concentrated sweets) clear liquid diet
  • Beware rapid glucose correction in patients with encephalopathy — may worsen cerebral edema and cause death due to transient hypo-osmolarity
  • Titration of insulin and dextrose is nurse-driven; electrolyte and volume management is provider-driven

Insulin Pump Guidance

  • If patient is on an insulin pump, ensure it is removed from patient and safely stored before initiating IV insulin.
  • Consult diabetes/endocrinology for pump management guidance (if available).
  • At transition: discontinue IV infusions 1 hour after patient's own pump is restarted.

Consults & Special Considerations

Neurology / Neurocritical Care: Consider for HHS patients with encephalopathy and suspected brain injury or cerebral edema. Rapid correction of glucose may worsen cerebral edema and cause death due to transient hypo-osmolarity.

Diabetes / Endocrinology: Consider if available. Required (if available) when insulin infusion exceeds 30 units/hr. Required for home insulin pump patients.

Titration roles: Insulin and dextrose titration is nurse-driven. Electrolyte management and volume resuscitation must be directed by the provider.

Post-Resolution: Routine Inpatient Diabetes Care

  • If eating: POC BG before meals and bedtime (QAc & QHs) and order prandial insulin
  • If NPO or on TPN/TEN: check POC BG Q4–6 hours
  • Order correctional insulin (formerly sliding scale) based on predicted sensitivity
  • Consider 3AM fingerstick the night of transition to evaluate basal insulin effects
  • Ensure daily basal insulin dose ordered within 24h of NOW basal insulin dose
  • Watch for decreasing insulin requirements as infectious precipitants and glucose toxicity improve

Safe IV → SQ Transition Details

Due to the short half-life of IV insulin (10–15 min), subcutaneous basal insulin (glargine) must be given before discontinuing the IV infusion to avoid rebound hyperglycemia.

  • Administer glargine as soon as it is delivered from Pharmacy, once HHS resolution criteria are met
  • Discontinue insulin and dextrose infusions 2 hours after glargine administered
  • If restarting patient's own pump: discontinue IV infusions 1 hour after pump restarted
  • Consult diabetes/endocrine if patient on home insulin pump (if available)

Insulin Resistance Definition

Insulin Resistant (use higher dose path):

  • BMI >30 OR receiving high-dose corticosteroids
  • AND eGFR >30

Not Insulin Resistant (use lower dose path):

  • BMI ≤30 AND NOT on high-dose corticosteroids
  • OR eGFR ≤30

High-dose corticosteroid thresholds:

  • Prednisone ≥ 40 mg/day
  • Methylprednisolone ≥ 30 mg/day
  • Dexamethasone ≥ 6 mg/day

High-Dose Steroid Thresholds

High-dose corticosteroid thresholds:

  • Prednisone ≥ 40 mg/day
  • Methylprednisolone ≥ 30 mg/day
  • Dexamethasone ≥ 6 mg/day

Insulin resistant pathway applies when: BMI >30 or receiving high-dose corticosteroids, and eGFR >30.

Not insulin resistant: BMI ≤30 and not on high-dose steroids, or eGFR ≤30.
